The Constitution Was Not a Suggestion

Published March 3, 2026

The Constitution was written with precision. Every clause, every check, every limitation on power was deliberate. The Founders did not draft a document they hoped future generations would follow. They established a framework they expected would bind them.

Yet somewhere along the way, we began treating that framework as advisory rather than binding.

The Constitution is not a suggestion. It is not a guideline open to interpretation based on convenience. It is the foundation of the republic, and it was designed to remain firm even when those in power would prefer it to bend.

The Lincoln Perspective

Abraham Lincoln governed during the greatest constitutional crisis in American history. The Union was fracturing, and there were voices on all sides urging him to set aside the Constitution in the name of expedience.

He refused.

Lincoln understood that the moment a republic treats its founding document as optional, it ceases to be a republic. The Constitution was not written to be convenient. It was written to limit power, protect liberty, and ensure that government remained accountable to the people it served.

Even in war, Lincoln believed the Constitution remained the anchor. Without it, he argued, there was nothing worth preserving.

Why This Still Matters

Today, we live in a time when the Constitution is often discussed as if it were a starting point for negotiation rather than a binding agreement. Political leaders speak of it selectively, citing it when useful and ignoring it when inconvenient.

But the Constitution does not function that way. It binds all parties equally, regardless of circumstance or preference. It defines what government may do and, just as importantly, what it may not do.

When we begin treating those limits as suggestions, we are not adapting to modern times. We are dismantling the only structure that prevents power from becoming arbitrary.

The Principle Behind the Shirt

The message "The Constitution Was Not a Suggestion" is blunt because it needs to be.

It is a reminder that the framework of this republic was established with intention. That the limits placed on government were not accidental. That the rights protected by that document are not granted by those in power. They are restrictions on power itself.

Principles only matter if they hold when they are inconvenient. The Constitution was written for precisely those moments.
